Problem

It answered the topic, not the question.

A keyword flattened distinct research needs into the same templated report. Users could neither shape the answer nor verify it.

Process

Three findings changed the product direction.

Customer behavior, product issues, and technical constraints pointed to the same gap: retrieval worked, but Deeli could not interpret intent or return an answer researchers could confidently use. Closing it took two control surfaces: a design spec the interface had to obey, and an eval suite that told me when the system’s judgment could be trusted.

The bet

Stop returning documents. Return the synthesis.

Nine cents a run bought grounded synthesis. I made the case to pay it: an unsourced number in front of a skeptical expert is the one failure the product cannot afford twice.

Control the interface with a spec.

Before any screens, I wrote design.md: five principles in priority order, then tokens. When two principles conflict, the earlier one wins, so layout arguments end in the doc instead of in a meeting.

Decision: keep a machine-readable twin, design.json, so every generated variant gets checked against the spec, not against taste.

Control the behavior with an eval.

A PRD cannot specify a non-deterministic output, so the eval suite is the spec: deterministic scorers for the unambiguous fields of the intent parser, an LLM judge only for the field that needed judgment. The nine golden cases distill the failure classes from the 263-issue tracker; the suite is small because each case is a class, not a sample.

Decision: when the eval flags a query as genuinely ambiguous, the product asks instead of guessing. Clarification came out of a diagnosed miss, not a feature brainstorm.

9-case suite against the live parser: 10 of 11 checks at 100%. The single miss sat on the field left to the model’s judgment. More on evals as the spec →

Solution

Four decisions turned search into a research partner.

I designed Deeli as a research instrument, not a chatbot: calm at entry, dense where evidence matters. Each decision closes one gap: express, aim, observe, verify.

Take the question, not the keyword.

Researchers could ask naturally, including fragments, long questions, and bilingual queries, without translating their intent into search syntax.

Decision: preserve the original query and show the system’s interpretation separately. Silent rewrites would break trust.

Observed: 70% of live queries used forms the old box could not support.

Learnings

Twenty-eight percent of queries were re-asks.

Twenty-six of 92 queries repeated an existing intent. Some reflected healthy exploration, but near-identical questions also produced inconsistent reports. Users were sometimes fishing for an answer they had already seen.

The shipped clarification helped researchers aim one report, but it did not preserve their corrected interpretation across repeated asks.

Next: turn one-time clarification into a persistent interpretation checkpoint. Let researchers confirm or correct entities and scope, then reuse that corrected intent on every re-ask.
